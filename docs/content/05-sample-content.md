## Module 1: "The Hidden Architecture of Music"

**Template:** Creative/Systems (combines Template 4 and Template 5 from the instructional design doc)
**Subject:** How harmony works in Western music
**Total Duration:** ~16 minutes (standard loop)
**Target Learner:** General audience, no prior music theory required

### Rationale for Template Choice

Harmony is both a system (interacting components produce emergent beauty) and a creative domain (the goal is not just understanding but eventually producing). The Creative/Systems hybrid template leads with immersion in the experience of harmony (RH), decomposes the system into intervals, consonance, and chords (LH), then returns to creative production -- composing a chord progression that tells an emotional story (RH). The Return stage is extended, as in the Creative template, because producing music is the deepest form of understanding it.

### ENCOUNTER (4 minutes)

#### Activity 1: The Hook (45 seconds)

**Screen:**

> *[Full-bleed background: a close-up photograph of a pianist's hands resting on keys, shot from above. The image is warm-toned, softly lit, intimate. No faces visible -- just hands and keys.]*
>
> **Two notes played together can make you feel safe.**
>
> **Two different notes can make you feel uneasy.**
>
> **Nobody taught you this. You knew it before you had words.**
>
> *[Below the text, a simple play button.]*

**Audio (auto-play on screen load, 8 seconds):**
A piano plays a perfect fifth (C and G together) -- a warm, open, stable sound. Two seconds of silence. Then a minor second (C and Db together) -- a tense, grinding, unsettled sound. Two seconds of silence.

**Interaction:** The learner listens, reads, and taps "Continue." No response required. The restraint is deliberate: the RH needs space to feel the contrast before any explanation arrives.

**Timing:** 45 seconds (30s minimum read + listen time, learner-paced beyond that).

**Image description for artist:** Top-down photograph of two hands resting on piano keys. The hands should be relaxed, not performing -- suggesting contemplation rather than virtuosity. Warm, amber lighting from the left. Shallow depth of field so the keys in the background blur. The overall mood is intimate and inviting, not intimidating.

**Adaptation:**
- *Novice:* Exactly as written. The hook assumes no musical knowledge.
- *Advanced:* Replace with: "You already know major sounds happy and minor sounds sad. But *why*? The answer involves mathematics that Pythagoras discovered 2,500 years ago -- and that your brain solves in milliseconds without trying."

### ANALYSIS (7 minutes)

#### Decomposition: The Building Blocks (2 minutes)

**Concept Card 1: Intervals**

> **An interval is the distance between two notes.**
>
> When you play two notes together (or one after another), the space between them is called an interval. Intervals are measured in "steps" or "semitones" -- the smallest distance between two keys on a piano.
>
> *[Diagram: A piano keyboard with C and G highlighted. An arc labeled "7 semitones" connects them. Below, a simple table:]*
>
> | Interval Name | Semitones | Example | Sound Quality |
> |---|---|---|---|
> | Unison | 0 | C to C | Identity |
> | Minor 2nd | 1 | C to Db | Tense, grinding |
> | Major 3rd | 4 | C to E | Bright, happy |
> | Perfect 5th | 7 | C to G | Open, stable |
> | Octave | 12 | C to C(high) | The "same note, higher" |

**Audio:** Each interval in the table has a small play button. Tapping plays the interval on a piano.

**Concept Card 2: Consonance and Dissonance**

> **Consonance** = intervals that sound stable, pleasant, "at rest."
> **Dissonance** = intervals that sound tense, unstable, "wanting to move."
>
> Consonance and dissonance are not good vs. bad. Music needs both. Consonance is home base; dissonance is the journey that makes coming home meaningful.
>
> *[Diagram: A horizontal spectrum. Left end labeled "Most consonant" (Unison, Octave, Perfect 5th). Right end labeled "Most dissonant" (Minor 2nd, Tritone). Other intervals distributed between.]*

**Audio:** A short (6-second) musical phrase that moves from consonance to dissonance and back to consonance. The learner hears the tension-resolution arc.

**Concept Card 3: Chords**

> **A chord is three or more notes played together.**
>
> The simplest chords are built by stacking intervals of a third (every other note in a scale):
>
> - **Major chord** = Root + Major 3rd + Minor 3rd (e.g., C-E-G). Sounds bright, stable, happy.
> - **Minor chord** = Root + Minor 3rd + Major 3rd (e.g., C-Eb-G). Sounds darker, reflective, melancholic.
> - **Diminished chord** = Root + Minor 3rd + Minor 3rd (e.g., C-Eb-Gb). Sounds tense, unstable, dramatic.
>
> *[Diagram: Three chord diagrams on a piano keyboard, each with the intervals labeled and color-coded.]*

**Audio:** Each chord type has a play button. C major, C minor, C diminished.

>

**Feedback (after submission):**

**Self-rating:** "Got it" / "Partially" / "Missed it"

**Item 2 -- Audio Recognition:**

> *[A play button. Plays a Perfect 5th (C-G).]*
>
> Which interval is this?
>
> (a) Minor 2nd
> (b) Major 3rd
> (c) Perfect 5th
> (d) Octave

**Correct answer:** (c) Perfect 5th

**Feedback:** "That's the Perfect 5th -- the wide, open, stable sound. Pythagoras's favorite ratio: 3:2."

**Item 3 -- Categorization (drag-and-drop):**

> Sort these intervals into "More Consonant" and "More Dissonant":
>
> *[Items to sort: Octave, Minor 2nd, Perfect 5th, Tritone, Major 3rd, Minor 7th]*

**Correct sort:**
- More Consonant: Octave, Perfect 5th, Major 3rd
- More Dissonant: Minor 2nd, Tritone, Minor 7th

**Feedback:** Per-item. Correct placements stick with a green pulse. Incorrect placements bounce back with a brief explanation. "The Major 3rd is consonant -- it's the bright interval that defines a major chord."

**Item 4 -- Chord Identification:**

> *[Play button. Plays a C minor chord.]*
>
> What type of chord is this?
>
> (a) Major
> (b) Minor
> (c) Diminished

**Correct answer:** (b) Minor

**Feedback:** "That's a minor chord -- built from a minor 3rd on the bottom and a major 3rd on top. Notice the darker, more reflective quality compared to major."

**Item 5 -- Active Recall:**

> What three intervals make up a major chord? (Name them in order from the root.)
>
> *[Free-text input.]*

**Target:** Root, Major 3rd, Minor 3rd (or equivalently: Root, Major 3rd, Perfect 5th)

**Feedback:** "A major chord is built from a Root, plus a Major 3rd above the root, plus a Minor 3rd above that (which creates a Perfect 5th from the root). Root + Major 3rd + Minor 3rd."

#### Elaborative Interrogation (1 minute)

**Prompt:**

> You learned that a Major chord sounds "bright and happy" while a Minor chord sounds "darker and melancholic." The only difference between them is the order of the thirds: Major chord = Major 3rd + Minor 3rd. Minor chord = Minor 3rd + Major 3rd.
>
> Why do you think swapping the order of two intervals changes the emotional feeling so dramatically? What is it about putting the smaller interval on the bottom?
>
> *[Free-text input, 4-6 sentences.]*

**Model answer (shown after submission):**

> When the smaller interval (Minor 3rd) is on the bottom, the lower notes are closer together, creating a "compressed" or "darkened" sound in the range where our ears are most sensitive to harmony. The larger interval on top opens up, but the foundation feels weighted and heavy. In a Major chord, the wider interval at the bottom creates a bright, expansive foundation. This is related to the overtone series -- Major 3rds align more closely with the natural harmonics of a vibrating string.
>
> *How does your explanation compare to this one?*

### RETURN (5 minutes) -- Extended for Creative template

#### Activity 1: The Reconnection (60 seconds)

**Screen:**

> *[The same piano-hands image from the Hook reappears. The same two audio clips play: the Perfect 5th, then the Minor 2nd.]*
>
> Listen again. At the beginning of this session, you felt the difference between these two sounds. Now you can name it.
>
> The first is a **Perfect 5th** -- a ratio of 3:2, one of the most consonant intervals in music. The second is a **Minor 2nd** -- just one semitone apart, maximum dissonance.
>
> But here's what matters: your ear knew this before your mind did. The analysis you just completed didn't *teach* you to hear the difference -- you already could. It gave you a vocabulary and a framework for something your right hemisphere has understood all along.

**Interaction:** The learner reads and listens. This is the "enriched return" -- the same experience, now with deeper understanding.

#### Activity 2: The Transfer Challenge (90 seconds)

**Screen:**

> Film composers use consonance and dissonance deliberately to control how you feel. Listen to these two short clips:
>
> *[Play button A: A 10-second clip of a warm, consonant string arrangement -- think the "safe home" theme from a film. Major chords, smooth voice leading, Perfect 5ths and Octaves prominent.]*
>
> *[Play button B: A 10-second clip of a tense, dissonant string arrangement -- think a thriller/horror score. Minor 2nds, Tritones, cluster chords.]*
>
> Using the vocabulary you just learned (intervals, consonance, dissonance, chord types), describe what each clip is doing musically and why it creates the emotional effect it does. Be specific -- name the intervals and chord qualities you hear.
>
> *[Free-text input, 6-8 sentences.]*

**Evaluation rubric (for LLM scoring):**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 4 (Excellent) | Correctly identifies specific intervals and chord types in both clips. Connects musical features to emotional effects using the consonance/dissonance framework. Notes the deliberate use of tension and resolution. |
| 3 (Proficient) | Identifies general consonance/dissonance distinction. Names at least 2 specific intervals or chord types. Connects to emotional effect. |
| 2 (Developing) | Identifies consonant vs. dissonant but does not name specific intervals. Emotional connection is vague. |
| 1 (Beginning) | Describes emotional effect but does not use musical vocabulary. |

**Model answer (shown after submission):**

> Clip A uses predominantly consonant intervals -- you can hear Perfect 5ths and Octaves in the bass, with Major 3rds creating a warm, major-key feeling. The chords move smoothly, staying in consonance. This creates a feeling of safety and warmth because your brain registers the simple ratios as stable and resolved.
>
> Clip B uses dissonant intervals -- Minor 2nds create a grinding tension, and you can hear a Tritone (the most dissonant interval, 6 semitones) lurking in the harmony. The chords are likely diminished or augmented, creating instability. This feels threatening because the dissonance signals "unresolved" to your brain -- something is wrong, something needs to change.

**Adaptation:**
- *Novice:* Provide the model answer in a side panel while the learner writes. "Use this as a guide."
- *Advanced:* Add a third clip that is more ambiguous (minor key, but with moments of consonance) and ask the learner to analyze the *emotional complexity* created by mixing consonance and dissonance.

#### Activity 3: Creative Synthesis -- Compose a Chord Progression (120 seconds)

**Screen:**

> *[A simple interactive chord selector: 8 buttons, each labeled with a chord name and quality.]*
>
> | C Major | D Minor | E Minor | F Major |
> | G Major | A Minor | B Diminished | C Major |
>
> *[Each button plays the chord when tapped. A "timeline" strip at the bottom has 4 slots. The learner drags chords into the slots to build a 4-chord progression. A "Play My Progression" button plays all 4 in sequence.]*
>
> **Create a 4-chord progression that tells a short emotional story.**
>
> Think about:
> - Where do you want to start? (Home base -- stable?)
> - Where do you want to go? (Tension? Sadness? Brightness?)
> - How do you want to end? (Resolved? Unresolved? Surprising?)
>
> After you build it, describe in one sentence what emotional story your progression tells.
>
> *[Free-text input below the chord builder: "My progression tells the story of..."]*

**Interaction:** The learner experiments by tapping chords, listening, dragging them into sequence, playing the sequence, revising. This is creative production -- the generation effect at maximum power. The free-text description forces articulation of the emotional intention, bridging LH verbal precision and RH musical intuition.

**Feedback:** No "correct answer." Instead, after submission, the system shows 3 famous chord progressions that use similar structures:

> **Your progression used [learner's chords].**
>
> Here are some famous progressions that explore similar territory:
> - **C - G - Am - F** (used in "Let It Be," "No Woman No Cry," dozens of others). This is the most common progression in pop music. It moves from stability (C major) through brightness (G major) to gentle sadness (A minor) and back toward home (F major).
> - **Am - F - C - G** (used in "What I've Done," "Zombie"). Same chords, different starting point. Beginning on the minor chord creates a darker, more searching quality.
>
> *Notice how the same building blocks, rearranged, tell completely different stories.*

**Adaptation:**
- *Novice:* Provide a "suggested starting point" -- pre-fill the first chord as C Major and the last as C Major, and ask the learner to fill in the two middle chords.
- *Advanced:* Expand the chord palette to include 7th chords, suspended chords, and key changes. Ask the learner to compose an 8-chord progression and write a paragraph explaining their harmonic choices.

## Module 2: "The Invisible Hand"

**Template:** Conceptual (Template 1 from the instructional design doc)
**Subject:** Supply and demand in economics
**Total Duration:** ~15 minutes (standard loop)
**Target Learner:** General audience, no prior economics knowledge required

### Rationale for Template Choice

Supply and demand is an abstract concept that must be understood, not merely memorized. The Conceptual template is ideal: begin with a vivid narrative that makes the learner *feel* scarcity and desire before any formal vocabulary appears, then decompose into the precise mechanics of curves, equilibrium, and elasticity, then return to apply the framework to novel situations the learner has never seen. The challenge is building a mental model, not executing a procedure.

---

### ENCOUNTER (3.5 minutes)

#### Activity 1: The Hook (40 seconds)

**Screen:**

> *[Full-bleed background: A single tulip, deep red, dramatically lit against a black background. The image is beautiful and slightly ominous.]*
>
> In 1637, a single tulip bulb sold for more than a house in Amsterdam. Skilled craftsmen earned 300 guilders a year. One bulb of the "Semper Augustus" variety sold for 10,000 guilders.
>
> Then, in February 1637, the market collapsed. In a single week, tulip prices fell by 90%. Fortunes evaporated. Families were ruined.
>
> What could make a flower worth more than a house -- and then worth almost nothing, overnight?

**Audio:** Optional ambient -- a low, sustained cello note that suggests both beauty and unease.

**Timing:** 40 seconds minimum. No time pressure.

**Image description for artist:** A single tulip, deep crimson with white flame markings (resembling the historical Semper Augustus variety). Shot against pure black background, dramatic chiaroscuro lighting from the upper left. The flower should be beautiful but the lighting should feel slightly foreboding -- this is beauty that carries danger. Reference Dutch Golden Age still-life painting for mood.

**Adaptation:**
- *Novice:* As written.
- *Advanced:* Replace with a more recent and complex example: "In March 2020, the price of hand sanitizer tripled overnight. Toilet paper vanished from shelves. Meanwhile, the price of jet fuel collapsed to nearly zero. The same week. The same economy. What invisible force was rearranging prices across the entire world?"

It blooms for two weeks and then it's gone. The moment enough people asked that question, the spell broke. Everyone tried to sell at once. Nobody wanted to buy.

### ANALYSIS (8 minutes)

#### Decomposition (2.5 minutes)

**Concept Card 1: Demand**

> **Demand** is the relationship between a product's price and the quantity people are willing to buy.
>
> The **Law of Demand:** As the price of something goes up, the quantity demanded goes down (all else being equal). As the price goes down, the quantity demanded goes up.
>
> This is intuitive: if coffee costs $2, you buy one every day. If it costs $20, you drink tea.
>
> *[Diagram: A standard demand curve. Price on the vertical axis, Quantity on the horizontal axis. The curve slopes downward from left to right. Two points are labeled: "Price $2, Quantity 100" and "Price $8, Quantity 30."]*

**Concept Card 2: Supply**

> **Supply** is the relationship between a product's price and the quantity producers are willing to sell.
>
> The **Law of Supply:** As the price of something goes up, the quantity supplied goes up (all else being equal). As the price goes down, the quantity supplied goes down.
>
> Also intuitive: if you can sell lemonade for $1, maybe you'll set up a stand on weekends. If you can sell it for $10, you'll set up a stand every day and hire your friends.
>
> *[Diagram: A standard supply curve. Price on vertical axis, Quantity on horizontal. The curve slopes upward from left to right. Two points labeled: "Price $2, Quantity 20" and "Price $8, Quantity 80."]*

**Concept Card 3: Equilibrium**

> **Equilibrium** is where the supply and demand curves cross -- the price at which the quantity buyers want to purchase equals the quantity sellers want to sell.
>
> - If the price is **above** equilibrium: sellers produce more than buyers want. Unsold goods pile up. This is a **surplus**. Sellers drop their prices to attract buyers.
> - If the price is **below** equilibrium: buyers want more than sellers produce. Shelves empty. This is a **shortage**. Sellers raise their prices (or buyers bid them up).
>
> The market tends to push toward equilibrium, like a ball rolling to the bottom of a bowl.
>
> *[Diagram: Supply and demand curves on the same graph. They cross at the equilibrium point. The equilibrium price and quantity are labeled. A shaded region above the equilibrium is labeled "Surplus" and a shaded region below is labeled "Shortage."]*

### ANALYSIS (9 minutes)

#### Decomposition (2.5 minutes)

**Concept Card 1: Variation**

> **Individuals in a population differ from one another.**
>
> No two organisms are identical (even identical twins have some differences). These differences can be physical (size, color, beak shape), behavioral (aggression, mating displays), or biochemical (disease resistance, metabolism).
>
> For natural selection to work, variation must be **heritable** -- passed from parents to offspring through genes. Not all variation is heritable (a scar from an accident isn't passed on), but much of it is.
>
> *[Diagram: A row of finches with visibly different beak sizes and shapes. Below each, a scale bar showing beak depth in millimeters. The variation is continuous -- not two types, but a range.]*

**Concept Card 2: Selection Pressure**

> **The environment poses challenges that not all individuals survive equally well.**
>
> Food scarcity, predators, disease, climate, competition for mates -- these are **selection pressures**. They create a situation where some traits are more advantageous than others.
>
> Example: On an island where the main food source is hard-shelled seeds, finches with thicker, stronger beaks can crack the seeds and eat. Finches with thin beaks go hungry. The thick-beaked finches survive and reproduce more. The thin-beaked finches die more often.
>
> *[Diagram: An island scene. Large seeds on the ground. Two finches: one with a thick beak cracking a seed (check mark), one with a thin beak unable to crack it (X mark). Simple, clear, not gruesome.]*

**Concept Card 3: Differential Reproduction**

> **Individuals with advantageous traits have more offspring.**
>
> This is the engine. It's not just about surviving -- it's about **reproducing**. A finch that survives to adulthood but never mates has zero evolutionary impact. A finch that survives, finds food easily (thanks to its thick beak), stays healthy, attracts a mate, and raises five chicks has a massive impact.
>
> Those five chicks inherit the thick-beak genes. In the next generation, thick beaks are more common. Over many generations, the average beak in the population gets thicker.
>
> **This is natural selection.** No one designed it. No one chose it. The environment selected which traits were passed on, automatically, through differential survival and reproduction.
>
> *[Diagram: A three-generation tree showing beak size inheritance. Generation 1: mix of thin and thick beaks. Generation 2: more thick beaks. Generation 3: mostly thick beaks. Accompanied by a simple bar chart showing the frequency shift.]*

**Concept Card 4: Adaptation (the outcome)**

> **Over many generations, natural selection produces adaptation: organisms become increasingly well-suited to their environment.**
>
> The finch's beak didn't change in one generation. It shifted slowly, over hundreds or thousands of generations, as each generation's thick-beaked individuals left slightly more offspring. The result: a species exquisitely adapted to cracking hard seeds.
>
> But adaptation is not "improvement" in an absolute sense. If the environment changes -- say the hard seeds disappear and are replaced by small insects -- the thick-beaked finches are suddenly at a disadvantage. Adaptation is always relative to the current environment.

May note that the speed of bacterial reproduction (generations in hours, not years) makes this evolution visible in human timescales. |
| 3 (Proficient) | Correctly traces the natural selection process. Explains overuse connection. Answer to question 3 is correct but less nuanced. |
| 2 (Developing) | Identifies natural selection as the mechanism but explanation is incomplete or partially inaccurate. |
| 1 (Beginning) | Does not connect the problem to natural selection, or uses Lamarckian reasoning ("bacteria learn to resist the antibiotic"). |

**Model answer:**

> 1. Bacterial populations have natural variation -- random mutations create some individuals with slight resistance to antibiotics. When an antibiotic is applied, it kills non-resistant bacteria (selection pressure). The resistant ones survive and reproduce (differential reproduction). Since bacteria reproduce every 20 minutes, the resistant population can explode within days. After enough cycles, the population is dominated by resistant bacteria.
>
> 2. Every time antibiotics are used, they create a selection event. Using them for viral infections (where they have no effect on the virus) still kills vulnerable bacteria in the body, giving any resistant bacteria a survival advantage. Using them in animal feed exposes billions of bacteria to low-level antibiotics constantly -- the perfect conditions for selecting resistance. More selection events = faster evolution of resistance.
>
> 3. A resistance-proof antibiotic is almost certainly impossible. Natural selection operates on variation, and mutation constantly generates new variation. No matter how the antibiotic works, random mutations will eventually produce a bacterium that can survive it. The question isn't whether resistance will evolve, but how quickly. The best strategy is not a "perfect antibiotic" but reducing selection pressure: use antibiotics only when necessary, use them at full strength (to kill ALL bacteria, not just the vulnerable ones), and develop diverse treatment strategies so no single resistance mutation confers a survival advantage.

**Adaptation:**
- *Novice:* Provide a structured template: "Step 1: What is the variation? Step 2: What is the selection pressure? Step 3: What is the result?" The learner fills in each step for question 1 only.
- *Advanced:* Add: "Design a hospital policy to slow the evolution of antibiotic resistance. Your policy should address at least three different points in the natural selection process."

## 4. Content Creation Style Guide

### 4.1 Tone of Voice

The Hemisphere voice is **warm but not patronizing, precise but not dry, curious but not breathless.**

**The voice should feel like:** A brilliant friend who is genuinely excited about what they know and respects you enough to share it properly -- not dumbed down, not showing off, just clear and alive.

**The voice should NOT feel like:** A textbook (distant, authoritative, dry). A children's TV presenter (over-enthusiastic, condescending). A TED talk (performed wonder, manufactured profundity). A clickbait article (hyperbolic, shallow).

**Specific guidelines:**

| Quality | Do | Don't |
|---------|-----|-------|
| **Warmth** | Address the learner directly. Use "you" naturally. Acknowledge difficulty and effort. | Use forced enthusiasm ("Awesome job!!! "). Patronize ("Don't worry, this is easy!"). |
| **Precision** | Use exact terminology when it matters. Define terms clearly. Be specific about quantities and relationships. | Hedge unnecessarily ("sort of," "kind of," "basically"). Be vague when clarity is possible. |
| **Curiosity** | Let genuine wonder come through. Ask real questions. Admit when something is surprising or counterintuitive. | Manufacture wonder ("Isn't that AMAZING?!"). Use rhetorical questions that feel hollow. |
| **Respect** | Trust the learner's intelligence. Present complexity without apology. Leave room for the learner's own thinking. | Simplify to the point of distortion. Tell the learner what to feel ("You should be amazed by this"). |
| **Honesty** | Acknowledge uncertainty, debate, and limits of current knowledge. Say "we don't fully understand this yet" when true. | Present contested ideas as settled fact. Oversimplify to avoid nuance. |
| **Rhythm** | Vary sentence length. Use short sentences for impact. Use longer sentences for flow and connection. | Write in monotone paragraph blocks. Use only short, choppy sentences. |

**Example -- rewriting a textbook passage:**

*Textbook version:* "Supply and demand is a fundamental economic model of price determination in a market. It postulates that, holding all else equal, in a competitive market, the unit price for a particular good, or other traded item such as labor or liquid financial assets, will vary until it settles at a point where the quantity demanded will equal the quantity supplied."

*Hemisphere version:* "Every price you've ever paid was shaped by two forces pulling in opposite directions. Buyers want the price to be low. Sellers want it to be high. The price where these two forces balance -- where the amount people want to buy exactly matches the amount sellers want to sell -- is called the equilibrium. It's the point where the tug-of-war reaches a stalemate."

---

### 4.2 Narrative Voice Guidelines

**Default voice: Second person ("you") for all stages.**

The learner is addressed directly. This creates immediacy and engagement. "You" is the most powerful word in educational writing because it activates the self-reference effect (Rogers, Kuiper, & Kirker, 1977) -- information related to the self is encoded more deeply.

**Encounter stage:** Second person, present tense, often with narrative past tense woven in.

> "In 1835, a 26-year-old noticed something about birds on an island. Today, you'll understand what he saw -- and why it mattered."

The learner is positioned as someone about to discover something. The narrative creates identification with the discoverer.

**Analysis stage:** Second person, present tense, instructional but not commanding.

> "An interval is the distance between two notes. When you hear a Perfect 5th, your brain registers a 3:2 frequency ratio."

The voice is slightly more formal, matching the analytical mode. But it remains direct and personal.

**Return stage:** Second person, present tense, reflective.

> "Look at this finch again. At the beginning of this session, it was a bird with an unusual beak. Now you can see the story written in that beak."

The voice invites the learner to step back and see what has changed in their understanding.

**When using historical narrative:** Shift to third person past tense for the historical content, but frame it in second person.

> "Darwin was 22 when he stepped onto the Beagle. *You* are about to follow the same trail of evidence he did."

**Never:** First person ("I"). The app does not have a persona or character. The voice is authoritative but not personified.

---

### 4.3 How to Write Encounter Narratives

#### The Principles

1. **Start with a person, a place, or a moment -- not a concept.** The Encounter is a story, not an introduction. The concept should emerge from the narrative, not precede it.

   - BAD: "Today we'll learn about supply and demand. Supply and demand is..."
   - GOOD: "In 1637, a single tulip bulb sold for more than a house in Amsterdam."

2. **Create a curiosity gap.** The narrative should open a question that the learner wants answered. The information gap (Loewenstein, 1994) is the engine of engagement.

   - BAD: "Harmony is an important concept in music theory."
   - GOOD: "Two notes played together can make you feel safe. Two different notes can make you feel uneasy. Nobody taught you this."

3. **Use concrete, sensory language.** Specific details engage mental simulation (Zwaan, 2004) and embodied cognition (Barsalou, 2008). The reader should see, hear, or feel something.

   - BAD: "The French Revolution was caused by economic inequality."
   - GOOD: "The price of bread consumed 88% of an average worker's wages. At Versailles, Marie Antoinette's annual clothing budget could have fed 1,000 families for a year."

4. **Anchor in emotion.** The Encounter should provoke wonder, surprise, indignation, awe, or productive confusion. Emotion signals to the brain: "This matters. Encode this."

   - The Hook should produce: "Wait, what?"
   - The Narrative should produce: "Tell me more."
   - The Prediction Prompt should produce: "Let me think about this."

5. **Do NOT resolve the mystery.** The Encounter opens questions; it does not answer them. The narrative should gesture toward the concept without naming it formally. The learner should finish the Encounter thinking: "I almost understand this -- I want to know more."

6. **Keep it short.** The entire Encounter is 3-4 minutes. Every sentence must earn its place. If a sentence doesn't create engagement, context, or curiosity, cut it.

#### Structural Template for Encounter Narratives

1. **The Hook (1-3 sentences):** A surprising fact, paradox, or question. Maximum impact, minimum words.
2. **The Scene (2-4 screens):** A story -- historical, contemporary, or hypothetical -- that embodies the concept. Characters, places, sensory details.
3. **The Turn (1 screen):** Something surprising, puzzling, or problematic. The moment where the curiosity gap opens.
4. **The Prediction (1 prompt):** "What do you think happened/is happening/will happen?" The learner becomes an active participant.
5. **The Bridge (1-2 sentences):** Gesture toward the formal concept. "The answer is one of the most powerful ideas in the history of science."

---

### 4.4 How to Write Analysis Content

#### The Principles

1. **Define terms precisely, once, and in plain English first.** Technical terms should be introduced with a clear definition in everyday language, followed by the formal term.

   - GOOD: "The distance between two notes is called an **interval**."
   - BAD: "An **interval** is the difference between two pitch frequencies, measured in cents or semitones, representing the ratio of their fundamental frequencies."

2. **One concept per card.** Each concept card should contain exactly one idea. If you need to explain two things, use two cards. Working memory is limited (Cowan, 2001 -- roughly 4 chunks). Respect it.

3. **Use progressive disclosure.** Introduce the simplest version first, then add complexity. Do not front-load qualifications, exceptions, and edge cases. "This is how it works. Later you'll learn when it doesn't."

4. **Connect every abstraction to a concrete example.** For every definition, provide an example. For every rule, show it in action. The concreteness fading principle (Goldstone & Son, 2005): concrete first, abstract second.

5. **Make the structure visible.** Use numbered lists, clear categories, and explicit labels. The LH excels at structured information. Give it structure.

6. **Write retrieval practice items that are specific and unambiguous.** Every question should have a defensible correct answer. Avoid trick questions. Difficulty should come from the content, not from confusing wording.

7. **Feedback should explain, not just evaluate.** "Wrong" is not feedback. "Not quite -- here's why" is feedback. Every piece of feedback should teach something, even (especially) when the learner was correct.

---

### 4.5 How to Write Return Prompts

#### The Principles

1. **Reconnect to the original narrative.** The first Return activity should always bring back the Encounter's hook or story. "Look at this again. What do you see now that you didn't before?" This is the core of the enriched return.

2. **Transfer challenges must be genuinely novel.** The scenario in the transfer challenge should be something the learner has NOT seen in the Encounter or Analysis. If the transfer challenge can be answered by recalling a specific example from the lesson, it's not a transfer challenge -- it's a recall question in disguise.

3. **Require application, not recitation.** The learner should use the framework to analyze something new, not repeat the framework back.

   - BAD: "What are the three ingredients of natural selection?" (This is recall, not transfer.)
   - GOOD: "A farmer uses the same pesticide for ten years and it becomes less effective. Explain why, using natural selection." (This is transfer.)

4. **Creative synthesis should be open-ended enough for genuine creativity, structured enough for assessment.** Provide constraints that channel creativity without prescribing the answer.

   - Too open: "Write something about supply and demand."
   - Too closed: "Explain why the price of coffee goes up when there's a drought."
   - Just right: "Explain supply and demand using a metaphor that has nothing to do with economics or markets."

5. **Reflection prompts should be genuinely metacognitive.** They should ask the learner to think about their own thinking, not just summarize the content.

   - BAD: "What did you learn today?" (This is recall.)
   - GOOD: "What surprised you? What do you still wonder about? What was hardest?" (These are metacognitive.)

---

### 4.6 Vocabulary Guidelines

**When to introduce technical terms:**
- AFTER the learner has experienced the concept, not before. Name the phenomenon only after the learner has felt it.
- At the moment of maximum need: when the learner is trying to articulate something and needs a word for it.

**How to introduce jargon:**
- Always define in plain language first, then provide the term.
  - "The price where the amount people want to buy equals the amount sellers want to sell is called the **equilibrium price**."
- Never assume a term is obvious. Even common academic words (e.g., "variable," "hypothesis," "function") may be unfamiliar.
- Bold the term on first use. Use it consistently thereafter without re-defining.

**Jargon budget:**
- A single lesson should introduce no more than 6-8 new technical terms.
- Every new term must appear at least 3 times in the session: once in the definition, once in practice, and once in the Return.
- If a term is introduced but never practiced or applied, cut it. It will become inert knowledge.

---

### 4.7 Length Guidelines by Content Type

| Content Type | Length | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Hook text | 1-3 sentences, max 60 words | Maximum impact, minimum words. The learner should be able to read it in 10 seconds. |
| Narrative Frame (per screen) | 1-3 sentences, max 50 words per screen | Screens should feel effortless to read. If the learner has to scroll, the screen has too much text. |
| Concept Card | 3-6 sentences, max 100 words + 1 diagram | One idea per card. Plain language first, formal term second. |
| Retrieval Practice prompt | 1-2 sentences, max 40 words | Clear, specific, unambiguous. The question itself should not be a reading comprehension challenge. |
| Feedback | 2-4 sentences, max 80 words | Brief but explanatory. Always explain *why*, not just *what*. |
| Transfer Challenge scenario | 2-4 sentences, max 80 words | Concise description of a novel situation. The scenario should be clear enough that the learner spends their effort on analysis, not on understanding the prompt. |
| Creative Synthesis prompt | 1-3 sentences, max 50 words | Clear constraints, open outcome. |
| Reflection prompt | 1-2 sentences, max 30 words | Simple, genuine, metacognitive. |
| Forward Glimpse | 2-3 sentences, max 50 words | A hook for next time. Create a curiosity gap that persists between sessions. |
| Model answer | 3-8 sentences, max 150 words | Thorough enough to learn from, concise enough to read quickly. |

## 6. Quality Checklist for Content Review

This checklist is used by content reviewers to evaluate any piece of content before publication. Every item should be checked. A module cannot ship with any "Fail" items in the Critical category.

---

### 6.1 Structural Integrity (Critical -- all must pass)

- [ ] **The full RH-LH-RH sequence is present.** The module contains all three stages: Encounter, Analysis, and Return. No stage is missing.
- [ ] **The correct order is maintained.** Encounter comes before Analysis, which comes before Return. The learner never encounters formal definitions or rules before experiencing the concept through narrative and context.
- [ ] **No premature abstraction.** The Encounter does NOT begin with a definition, a rule, a formula, or a technical term. It begins with a story, a question, a paradox, or an experience.
- [ ] **The Analysis stage does not stand alone.** The module does not end after the Analysis stage. A Return stage is present and substantive (not a token afterthought).
- [ ] **The Return reconnects to the Encounter.** The first activity in the Return explicitly revisits the Hook or Narrative from the Encounter, re-presenting it with the enriched understanding from the Analysis.
- [ ] **Transitions are marked.** Clear verbal and UI transition cues exist between Encounter-to-Analysis and Analysis-to-Return.
- [ ] **Stage timing is within bounds.** Encounter: 3-4 minutes. Analysis: 6-10 minutes. Return: 3-5 minutes. Total: 12-18 minutes.

---

### 6.2 Encounter Quality (Critical)

- [ ] **The Hook creates a genuine curiosity gap.** The Hook presents a surprising fact, paradox, or question that the learner will genuinely want to answer. It is NOT a bland statement of topic importance ("Today we'll learn about...").
- [ ] **The Narrative is a story, not an explanation.** The Narrative Frame follows a character, a situation, or an event. It has scene-setting, tension, and a turn. It is NOT a list of facts or a textbook introduction in narrative clothing.
- [ ] **Concrete, sensory language is used.** The learner can visualize what is described. Specific details are present (numbers, names, places, sensory descriptions). Abstract language is minimal.
- [ ] **Emotional engagement is present.** The Encounter provokes wonder, curiosity, surprise, empathy, or productive confusion. The learner feels something, not just learns something.
- [ ] **A prediction prompt is included.** At least one point in the Narrative asks the learner to predict, guess, or think before seeing the answer.
- [ ] **The concept is NOT fully explained in the Encounter.** The Encounter gestures toward the concept but does not name it formally or explain it fully. The learner finishes the Encounter wanting to know more.
- [ ] **The Spatial Overview is present.** A concept map showing the topic's relationship to the broader domain is provided.

---

### 6.3 Analysis Quality (Critical)

- [ ] **Key concepts are decomposed into distinct, labeled components.** The concept is broken into 3-6 clearly defined parts, each presented on its own concept card.
- [ ] **Definitions are precise and in plain language.** Every technical term is defined clearly. Plain language comes first, formal term second.
- [ ] **Retrieval practice is the primary activity.** At least 60% of Analysis time is spent on retrieval (free recall, cued recall, categorization, sequencing), not on passive reading or watching.
- [ ] **Free recall comes before cued recall.** When both are present, the more difficult retrieval format is offered first, with the option to downgrade.
- [ ] **Feedback is explanatory, not just evaluative.** Every feedback message explains WHY the answer is correct or incorrect, not just THAT it is.
- [ ] **Feedback is growth-oriented.** Errors are framed productively ("Not quite -- here's why"). No punitive language, no "hearts" or "lives" lost.
- [ ] **A worked example or fading sequence is present (where applicable).** For procedural or analytical content, a full worked example with self-explanation prompts is provided, with fading planned for subsequent sessions.
- [ ] **At least one elaborative interrogation prompt is included.** A "why" question that bridges from analytical knowledge toward deeper understanding.
- [ ] **No more than 6-8 new terms are introduced.** Every new term appears at least 3 times in the session.

---

### 6.4 Return Quality (Critical)

- [ ] **The Reconnection revisits the original Encounter.** The same image, narrative element, or question from the Hook is explicitly re-presented with enriched understanding.
- [ ] **The Transfer Challenge uses a genuinely novel scenario.** The scenario is NOT one that appeared in the Encounter or Analysis. The learner must apply the concept to something they have not seen.
- [ ] **Transfer requires application, not recitation.** The learner cannot answer the Transfer Challenge by quoting the lesson back. They must reason with the concept.
- [ ] **A creative synthesis activity is present.** The learner creates something: a metaphor, an explanation, an analysis, a design, a connection. Multiple format options are offered.
- [ ] **A metacognitive reflection prompt is present.** The learner is asked to reflect on their own learning process, not just on the content.
- [ ] **A confidence rating is collected.** The learner rates their understanding (1-5).
- [ ] **A Forward Glimpse creates a curiosity gap for next time.** The module ends with a teaser that makes the learner want to continue.

---

### 6.5 Adaptation (Important)

- [ ] **Novice, intermediate, and advanced adaptations are specified.** The module describes how it changes for learners at different levels.
- [ ] **Novice adaptations provide more scaffolding, not less content.** Novice learners see the same concepts with more support (guided prompts, sentence starters, model answers in view), not a simplified version that cuts important content.
- [ ] **Advanced adaptations provide more challenge, not more content.** Advanced learners face harder transfer challenges, less scaffolding, and more cross-topic integration, not just more facts.
- [ ] **Retrieval practice items exist at all four difficulty levels.** For each key concept, items are available at Level 1 (recognition), Level 2 (recall), Level 3 (application), and Level 4 (transfer).

---

### 6.6 Media and Production (Important)

- [ ] **Image descriptions are precise enough for an artist to execute.** Descriptions include subject, composition, lighting, mood, color palette, and style reference. No vague descriptions ("a nice image of the topic").
- [ ] **Audio descriptions specify content, duration, and purpose.** Audio elements describe what is heard, how long it lasts, and what emotional or cognitive function it serves.
- [ ] **All images have alt-text specifications.** Accessibility is built in from the start.
- [ ] **Diagrams in the Analysis stage are labeled, simplified, and structural.** Analysis diagrams are not decorative -- they highlight relationships and structure. They are NOT realistic illustrations (those belong in the Encounter).
- [ ] **Interactive elements specify the exact interaction pattern.** For drag-and-drop, sorting, or matching activities, the exact items, targets, and feedback patterns are specified.

---

### 6.7 Tone and Voice (Important)

- [ ] **The tone is warm but not patronizing.** The content respects the learner's intelligence. No "Great job!" or "This is easy!"
- [ ] **The tone is precise but not dry.** Technical content is accurate and specific, but written in a voice that sounds human, not institutional.
- [ ] **The voice uses second person ("you") consistently.** First person ("I") is not used. Third person is used only for historical narrative characters.
- [ ] **Sentence length varies.** Short sentences for impact. Longer sentences for explanation and flow. No monotone paragraph blocks.
- [ ] **The Encounter voice is narrative and evocative.** The Analysis voice is clear and instructional. The Return voice is reflective and expansive. The tonal shift across stages is evident.

---

### 6.8 Learning Science Compliance (Important)

- [ ] **The module implements at least 5 of the following evidence-based strategies:** Retrieval practice, spaced repetition items, interleaving hooks, elaborative interrogation, dual coding (verbal + visual), concrete examples before abstractions, generation effect (learner produces), worked example fading, and metacognitive reflection.
- [ ] **No pedagogical anti-patterns are present:** Definition-first introduction, drill without context, passive re-reading presented as "studying," gamification as primary motivation, information overload, pseudo-engagement (manufactured enthusiasm).
- [ ] **Spaced review items are specified.** Each key concept has review items at multiple difficulty levels, with context variations for repeated encounters.
- [ ] **Cross-topic links are identified.** The module specifies which previously studied (or future) topics connect to the current one, for use in interleaving and Return-stage connection-making.

---

### 6.9 Content Accuracy (Critical)

- [ ] **All factual claims are accurate and sourced.** Historical dates, scientific findings, quotations, and statistics are verified. Simplification is acceptable; inaccuracy is not.
- [ ] **Simplifications are honest.** Where the content simplifies for pedagogical purposes, it does not misrepresent the underlying reality. If the simplified version could lead to a misconception, a note is included ("This is simplified -- the full picture is more complex, and you'll encounter it later").
- [ ] **Quotations are attributed and accurate.** No fabricated quotes. Historical dialogue presented as narrative ("He wrote in his notebook...") is based on documented sources.
- [ ] **Model answers are thorough and defensible.** Model answers for transfer challenges and elaborative interrogation are reviewed by a subject-matter expert.
